## Polyhedral Unmixing: Bridging Semantic Segmentation with Hyperspectral Unmixing via Polyhedral-Cone Partitioning 

Antoine Bottenmuller , Etienne Decencière and Petr Dokladal 

Mines Paris, PSL University, Centre for Mathematical Morphology (CMM) 77300 Fontainebleau, France 

{antoine.bottenmuller, etienne.decenciere, petr.dokladal}@minesparis.psl.eu 

Code: https://github.com/antoine-bottenmuller/polyhedral-unmixing 

Abstract 

Semantic segmentation and hyperspectral unmixing are two central problems in spectral image analysis. The former assigns each pixel a discrete label corresponding to its material class, whereas the latter estimates pure material spectra, called endmembers, and, for each pixel, a vector representing material abundances in the observed scene. Despite their complementarity, these two problems are usually addressed independently. This paper aims to bridge these two lines of work by formally showing that, under the linear mixing model, pixel classification by dominant materials induces polyhedral-cone regions in the spectral space. We leverage this fundamental property to propose a direct segmentation-to-unmixing pipeline that performs blind hyperspectral unmixing from any semantic segmentation by constructing a polyhedral-cone partition of the space that best fits the labeled pixels. Signed distances from pixels to the estimated regions are then computed, linearly transformed via a change of basis in the distance space, and projected onto the probability simplex, yielding an initial abundance estimate. This estimate is used to extract endmembers and recover final abundances via matrix pseudo-inversion. Because the segmentation method can be freely chosen, the user gains explicit control over the unmixing process, while the rest of the pipeline remains essentially deterministic and lightweight. Beyond improving interpretability, experiments on three real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach when associated with appropriate clustering algorithms, and show consistent improvements over recent deep and non-deep state-of-the-art methods. 

Keywords: hyperspectral imaging, classification, blind unmixing, convex partition, polyhedral cone 

1 Introduction 

In remote sensing, hyperspectral imaging systems acquire information about an object or a scene without physical contact [ 4, 32 ]. They capture discretized spectral signatures of materials, typically representing their absorbance or reflectance, over tens to hundreds of contiguous, narrow bands [ 34 ]. A hyperspectral dataset is therefore a particular type of image where, for d bands, each pixel is an element in the Euclidean space Rd, referred to as the spectral space [7 ], where the i-th entry is the spectral response at the i-th band. Because of limited spatial resolution or the intrinsic heterogeneity of the observed medium, the spectral signature of a single pixel results from a mixture of multiple materials [ 66 , 8]. The spectral signatures of pure materials are referred to as endmembers , and their proportions within a pixel are 

abundances [66 , 72 , 8 ]. The relationship between observed signatures, endmembers, and abundances is defined by a mixing model . We focus on the standard widely-used linear mixing model (LMM) [ 31 , 47 ], which neglects nonlinear interactions by modelling each pixel as a linear combination of endmembers weighted by their abundances. Given a dataset Y , hyperspectral image analysis typically addresses two central problems [7, 58]: 

1. Semantic segmentation (or Classification [7 ]). It assigns each pixel of Y a label representing its material class, yielding a classification map C [30, 69]. 

2. Hyperspectral unmixing . It estimates pure material signatures (endmembers M ) and their pixelwise fractions (abundances A) [20, 72, 74]. 1

Research on semantic segmentation for hyperspectral image classification spans supervised and unsu-pervised, deep and non-deep, techniques [ 41 , 55 , 70 ]. A particular case is dominant-material segmentation (or classification) [ 15 , 22 ], where the assigned class corresponds to the material with maximal abundance in each mixed pixel. We adopt this setting in this paper. Hyperspectral unmixing is more demanding, as it seeks both M and A. It has been approached via geometrical, statistical, nonnegative matrix factorization, and deep-learning methods [ 8 , 72 , 5 ]. Recent works include autoencoder-based models and deep unrolling of classical algorithms, frequently achieving stronger performance [66]. We place ourselves in the blind setting, where no prior information about pure materials is provided, and only unsupervised techniques are allowed [ 65 , 66 ]. Optionally, only the number of materials is known. In hyperspectral semantic segmentation, the methods often leverage clustering or classical unsupervised techniques performed in the spectral space (e.g., k-means, Gaussian mixture models), with spatial priors [ 69 ]. Classical blind linear unmixing follows a two-step pipeline [ 82 , 72 ]: (i) endmember extraction, then (ii) abundance estimation. Dominant-material labels follow by taking the arg max of the abundances in each pixel [ 29 ]. A preprocessing step is also often applied [ 31 ]. Some statistical and deep learning-based approaches jointly estimate M and A [8, 66]. Despite their complementarity, these two problems are usually addressed independently [ 31 , 47 ]. While dominant-material segmentation ˆC is directly derived from unmixing outputs ˆA and ˆM [ 29 ], the reverse direction, i.e., performing unmixing from Y and ˆC, requires a model that, to our knowledge, has not been formulated yet. Contributions. We propose a segmentation-to-unmixing model through a new blind linear unmixing approach by rethinking the standard pipeline, bridging semantic segmentation with hyperspectral unmixing. We prove that, under the LMM, dominant-material classification induces a partition of the spectral space into polyhedral cones. Leveraging this property, and given any semantic segmentation, we fit a polyhedral-cone partition to the labeled pixels; compute signed distances from pixels to the corresponding convex regions; and project onto the probability simplex to obtain an initial abundance estimate. This estimate is then used to recover endmembers and final abundances. This reversed-pipeline strategy makes segmentation drive unmixing, giving users explicit control through the choice of segmentation method, while the subsequent steps are lightweight and reproducible. Beyond improving interpretability, the approach opens new paths to hyperspectral unmixing. Experiments on three widely used real datasets, namely Samson, Urban and Jasper Ridge, show consistent improvements over recent deep and non-deep baselines. Contrarily to existing blind unmixing methods incorporating segmentation information, which use clustering to select candidate pure pixels and then apply standard endmember extraction [ 42 , 43 , 33 , 78 ], our model derives a direct segmentation-to-unmixing pipeline based on a fundamental polyhedral-based geometric property of dominant-material regions under the LMM that we formalize and prove in this paper. While our method is compatible with supervised segmentation methods, we focus here on unsupervised pixel classification (clustering) algorithms to perform blind unmixing. 2The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 recalls the fundamentals of blind unmixing under the LMM, reviews benchmark methods and highlights the standard processing chain. Section 3 establishes the polyhedral-cone partitioning property and presents our segmentation-driven unmixing algorithm. Section 4 reports experimental results and their comparisons to eight baseline methods. Section 5 concludes and outlines future directions. 

2 Related Work 

This section aims to give background knowledge about blind hyperspectral unmixing under linear mixing assumption, and fixes notations used throughout the paper. 

2.1 Linear Mixing Model 

The LMM models the observed data Y as linear combinations of endmembers M weighted by their abundances A. If an image contains n pixels, each represented by a spectrum of d bands, and is assumed to involve m material endmembers, the LMM is then formulated by the matrix equation 

Y = M A + E, (1) where Y ∈ Rd×n is the matrix stacking the n pixel spectra; M ∈ Rd×m is the matrix of the m endmember spectra; A ∈ Rm×n is the abundance matrix (every real Aij is the proportion of the i-th endmember in the j-th pixel); and E ∈ Rd×n is a residual zero-mean additive Gaussian noise with low variance [ 20 ]. A

satisfies, for all j = 1 , . . . , n , { Aij ≥ 0 for i = 1 , . . . , m ∑mi=1 Aij = 1 . (2) Constraints (2) ensure that the data Y , when neglecting noise E in (1) , lie within the (m − 1) -simplex defined by the endmembers M in the spectral space [72, 8]. 

2.2 Existing Approach Overview 

In the blind scenario, the objective of hyperspectral unmixing is to recover both the matrices M and A in (1) ,whose estimations are denoted by ˆM and ˆA, respectively, using only the observed data Y [65 , 66 ]. This constitutes a blind source separation (BSS) problem [ 82 , 75 , 9]. Numerous algorithms have been developed to address the blind linear unmixing problem, which, based on three comprehensive surveys [ 66 , 72 , 8], can be broadly grouped into four main categories. 

Geometrical approaches focus on endmember extraction by exploiting the geometry of the data cloud Y and the endmember simplex to determine M [8]. They fall into two main groups: (i) minimum volume (MV) algorithms, which fit the smallest-volume enclosing simplex, such as the minimum volume simplex analysis (MVSA) [ 37 ] or the simplex identification via split augmented Lagrangian (SISAL) [ 28 ]; and (ii) pure pixel (PP) algorithms, which assume endmembers are present among the pixels and identify them directly, such as the vertex component analysis (VCA) [50]. 

Nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) finds ˆM and ˆA by minimizing the squared Frobenius norm ∥Y − ˆM ˆA∥2 

> F

with an additional regularization term on ˆM , under nonnegativity constraints and abundance constraints (2) [23 ]. Notable variants include the minimum volume constrained-nonnegative matrix factorization (MVC-NMF) [ 48 ], which incorporates a minimum-volume penalty, and the iterative constrained endmembers (ICE) algorithm [ 3 ], which regularizes using the cumulative distance between endmembers. 

Statistical approaches incorporate prior knowledge to regularize the solution space and perform maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference, typically under Gaussian likelihoods [ 8, 19 ]. Under Gaussian noise assumption, this leads to constrained optimization problems similar in form to NMF [ 72 ]. Examples include sparse Bayesian learning [ 17 ], and dependent component analysis (DECA) [ 49 ] which assumes a mixture of Dirichlet densities as a prior for the abundances. 

Deep learning methods often recast classical unmixing models into deep neural architectures [ 5 , 20 ]. Autoencoder variants map abundances to the latent space (the code) and endmembers to the linear decoder weights, with training typically minimizing a regularized Frobenius loss similar in form to NMF [ 53 , 54 ]; popular examples include EndNet [ 52 ] and the minimum simplex convolutional network (MiSiCNet) [ 63 ]. Unrolling approaches embed iterative solvers (e.g., the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) or NMF) into trainable networks, such as ADMMNet [83] or SNMF-Net [76]. 3M2 M2 M2

3 Proposed Unmixing Method 

We state in this section our main theorem on the geometric structure of the spectral space under the LMM, and derive from it a new linear hyperspectral unmixing method driven by semantic segmentation in the blind setting. The proofs of Lemmas 1 and 2 and of Theorem 1 are provided in Appendix A. 

3.1 The Polyhedral-Cone Partition Theorem 

We work in the Euclidean space Rd modeling the spectral space, and begin with the definitions of the following fundamental geometric objects. 

Definition 1 (Halfspace) . A subset H of Rd is a (closed) halfspace if there exist w ∈ Rd∗ and b ∈ R such that 

H = {x ∈ Rd ∣∣ ⟨x, w ⟩ ≤ b} . (4) It is exactly one of the two closed subsets of Rd resulting in the split of the space by an affine hyperplane [ 44 ]. 

Definition 2 (Polyhedron) . A (convex) polyhedron, or polyhedral set , of Rd is the intersection of finitely-many closed halfspaces of Rd [13]. 4Definition 3 (Polyhedral Cone) . A polyhedral cone of Rd is a polyhedron whose boundary hyperplanes all pass through the origin 0d (i.e., b = 0 ) [2, 1]. Polyhedral sets, as intersections of convex sets, are then also convex. 3.2 Method Overview 

Our method inverts the standard pipeline (Figure 2), with segmentation as entry point and Theorem 1 as the geometric backbone. It comprises three main stages: 1. Polyhedral-cone partitioning: the pixels are classified by a clustering or unsupervised segmenta-tion method, yielding a classification map ˆC, and a polyhedral-cone partition of the spectral space is fitted over the labeled data. 2. Initial abundance estimate: a first abundance estimate ˆA′ is computed from the space partition using the signed distances from the data to the polyhedral-cone regions. 3. Endmember and abundance recovery: from ˆA, endmembers ˆM are extracted and final abundances ˆA are recovered by matrix pseudo-inversion. 3.3 Polyhedral-Cone Partitioning 

The first stage aims to find a polyhedral-cone partition of the spectral space that best separates the data labeled by any chosen (unsupervised) semantic segmentation method. It is composed of three sub-steps, illustrated in Figure 3: 1. (A. → B.) Preprocessing . A preliminary step optionally maps Y to Y ′. We may (i) normalize spectral luminance by projecting the data onto the unit sphere S of Rd to mitigate shadows and illumination effects [88], and (ii) reduce space dimensionality to d′ < d via PCA [31, 66]. 2. (B. → C.) Semantic segmentation . A hyperspectral semantic segmentation method (via pixel classification or clustering) [ 36 ] is applied to Y ′ to obtain a classification map ˆC into m classes, where 

m is either fixed or estimated [ 14 , 8, 45 ]. For instance, Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) [ 62 , 79 ]provide a flexible clustering tool for non-homogenous spectral data [38]. 3. (C. → D.) Separation hyperplanes . Leveraging Theorem 1 and the Hyperplane Separation Theorem [ 68 ], linear hyperplanes that best pairwise separate the m class clusters are estimated. 4 Experiments 

We evaluate our unmixing method in the blind setting on three real datasets, and compare the results with eight state-of-the-art unmixing algorithms. The visualizations of the results (estimated abundance maps and endmembers for unmixing experiments, and classification maps for robustness evaluation to different clustering methods) are provided in Appendix B. 

4.1 Hyperspectral Datasets 

We consider three widely-used real hyperspectral datasets, all publicly available with their ground truths [88, 84]: 

• Samson [85 ]. The Samson dataset is a 95 ×95 pixels sub-image of a larger dataset. It uses a total of 156 spectral bands spanning from 401 to 889 nm. Three main materials are observed in the scene: #1 Soil, #2 Tree and #3 Water. 

• Jasper Ridge [86 ]. Jasper Ridge is a 100 ×100 pixels sub-image of a larger dataset captured by the AVIRIS sensor [ 26 ]. It uses 224 spectral bands spanning from 380 to 2500 nm. 26 noisy bands are removed before processing, leaving a total of 198 bands. Four materials are observed in the scene: #1 Soil, #2 Tree, #3 Water and #4 Road. 

• Urban-6 [59 ]. The Urban-6 dataset is a 307 ×307 pixels image captured by the HYDICE sensor [ 67 ]. It uses 210 spectral bands spanning from 400 to 2500 nm. 48 of them are removed as a pre-processing step due to atmospheric noise effects, leaving a total of 162 bands. The dataset contains six materials: #1 Asphalt, #2 Grass, #3 Tree, #4 Roof, #5 Metal and #6 Dirt. 

4.2 Benchmark Methods 

We compare to eight strong baselines across three families: 

• Geometrical : one pure-pixel (PP) algorithm, VCA [ 50 ]; and one minimum-volume (MV) algorithm, SISAL [6], among the most effective [66]. 

• NMF : a quadratic minimum volume one, NMF-QMV [ 87 ], which is a strong benchmark NMF algorithm [66]. 

• Deep learning-based : three autoencoders, (i) Endnet [ 52 ], which includes a loss function with several terms, (ii) MiSiCNet [ 63 ], which incorporates geometrical information, and (iii) a recent cascaded spatial-spectral Mamba-based model (UNMamba) [ 16 ]; and one deep unrolling model, ADMMNet [ 83 ], which embed the ADMM [ 12 ] solver into a trainable architecture. We also test Entropic Descent Archetypal Analysis (EDAA) [ 88 ] which uses an entropic descent algorithm to solve the Archetypal Analysis problem [66]. MiSiCNet has been shown to outperform prior deep and non-deep methods [ 63 , 88 ], such as CyCUNet [ 25 ], uDAS [ 61 ] and UnDIP [ 64 ]. EDAA outperforms MiSiCNet [ 88 ]. 4.6 Impact of input segmentation on unmixing performance 

Since our unmixing method naturally depends on the input segmentation, we assess here how segmentation quality affects unmixing performance. Table 4 reports the unmixing results obtained with our segmentation-to-unmixing model over six input classification maps: the ground truth and the outputs of five clustering algorithms, namely k-Means, GMM, Spectral Clustering [ 51 ], Self-supervised Double-Structure Transformer (SDST) [ 40 ], and Elastic Graph Fusion Subspace Clustering (EGFSC) [ 81 ]. For each input classification map, we report its accuracy (Acc.), together with the abundance RMSE and endmember SAD produced by our unmixing method, averaged over materials. These results highlight the importance of the clustering model chosen to unmix a dataset, especially in challenging scenarios like Urban-6. Overall, EGFSC provides the best performance among the compared methods, both in terms of segmentation accuracy (Acc.) and unmixing quality (SAD and RMSE), except on Samson for which a GMM is associated with better unmixing results despite providing the lowest segmentation accuracy. The results, in particular for Samson and Jasper Ridge, also show that the ground-truth segmentation does not necessarily lead to the best unmixing performance: although it provides the correct class labels, the separating hyperplanes learned by the SVM [57] are determined by margin maximization rather than by the underlying physical mixing process. As a result, the induced decision boundaries may be suboptimal for endmember and abundance estimation. We also evaluated our method on ground-truth segmentation maps corrupted by different levels of label noise, using the same experimental protocol as in [ 11 ]. Table 5 shows that our method remains robust to uniformly distributed random label noise. This can be explained by the well-known robustness of SVMs to uniformly misclassified samples, since the solution mainly depends on support vectors, which may remain stable or be only mildly perturbed under such noise. Interestingly, introducing a small amount of noise can even improve the predictions, as observed for Jasper Ridge and Urban-6. One possible explanation is that the noise leads to a better balance between classes and relaxes the position of the support vectors. However, the previous experiment (see Table 4) suggests that the method remains sensitive to the geometry of the class regions in the classification map, since SVMs are particularly influenced by boundary pixels. 

5 Conclusion 

We introduced a new blind linear unmixing approach that bridges semantic segmentation and hyperspectral unmixing by inverting the standard pipeline. It estimates endmembers and abundances from any input classification map. The core result is a theorem showing that, under the linear mixing model, dominant-material regions partition spectral space into polyhedral cones. We leverage this property by using signed distances to polyhedral cones determined by support vector machines to perform unmixing in the blind setting. The method is lightweight, easy to deploy, and essentially deterministic. It has a single hyperparameter controlling initial abundance saturation. On Samson, Jasper Ridge and Urban-6, it delivers consistent improvements over eight deep and non-deep baselines, especially for endmember recovery, while remaining competitive in abundance estimation and in computation time. Its effectiveness, however, depends on the quality of the classification map given as input. Our theorem and proposed segmentation-driven pipeline open new directions for hyperspectral unmixing. We could exploit cone–endmember relations for direct endmember identification or incorporate the polyhedral-cone prior into deep architectures to guide learning and further improve unmixing quality.
