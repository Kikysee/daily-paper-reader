Abstract 

Monocular building height estimation from optical imagery is important for urban morphology characterization, yet it remains highly challenging due to the ambiguity of inferring height from image appearance, the large variation in building morphology across cities, and the long-tailed distribution of building heights. Recently launched PhiSat-2 imagery is an open-access data source that is potentially well suited to this task because of its global coverage, 4.75 m spatial resolution, and seven-band spectral obser-vations. However, its potential for monocular building height estimation has not yet been systematically evaluated, and dedicated datasets and task-oriented methods are still lacking. To address this gap, a PhiSat-2–Height dataset (PHDataset) was constructed and a Two-Stream Ordinal Network (TSONet) was proposed. PHDataset integrates global PhiSat-2 imagery with open-source building height labels, and 9,475 co-registered patch pairs in 26 cities worldwide were generated after data processing. TSONet improves building height estimation by incorporating an auxiliary footprint segmentation stream within a multi-task framework. Selective cross-task feature interaction is enabled by a Cross-Stream Exchange Module (CSEM), through which complementary cues from the footprint segmentation and height estimation streams are adaptively exchanged to enhance footprint-aware structural guidance. Meanwhile, a Feature-Enhanced Bin Refinement (FEBR) module is designed to perform coarse-to-fine ordinal query refinement with multi-level features, so that feature-enhanced ordinal height modeling and more accurate height prediction can be achieved. Extensive experiments were conducted against representative methods on PHDataset, and TSONet achieved the best overall performance, reducing MAE and RMSE by over 13.2% and 9.7%, respectively, while improving IoU and F1-score by over 14.0% and 10.1%. The e ff ectiveness of CSEM, FEBR, and the joint use of ordinal regression and footprint assistance was fur-ther verified by ablation studies. Additional analyses further indicate that PhiSat-2 benefits monocular building height estimation through its balanced combination of building-relevant spatial detail and multispectral observations. Overall, the potential of PhiSat-2 imagery for monocular building height estimation was confirmed, and a new dedicated dataset together with an e ff ective method was provided for future research. The PHDataset and TSONet are publicly available at Hugging Face and GitHub. 

Keywords: Monocular height estimation, Ordinal regression, Multi-task learning, PhiSat-2 

1. Introduction 

Accurate building height estimation (BHE) is fundamental to characterizing the built environment, as it provides critical vertical information for a more complete representation of ur-ban morphology, including attributes such as building volume and floor area ratio (Li et al., 2020b; Cai et al., 2023). Reli-able building height data support a wide range of applications, including energy-use analysis (Godoy-Shimizu et al., 2018), population estimation (Tomás et al., 2016), climate adapta-tion (Li et al., 2025), and disaster risk assessment (Tian et al., 2025). However, large-scale, fine-grained, and up-to-date BHE remains challenging. Traditional surveying techniques, such as field campaigns, can provide accurate measurements, but they

These limitations have driven growing interest in estimating building height from satellite remote sensing imagery, which off ers an e ffi cient and scalable solution for large-area mapping. A variety of remote sensing approaches have been explored for BHE. Stereo optical imagery has been widely used to re-construct three-dimensional urban information (Chen et al., 2023a), but it is often di ffi cult to acquire such data with both broad spatial coverage and high temporal frequency. Space-borne LiDAR observations provide highly accurate vertical measurements (Ma et al., 2023), yet their sparse sampling pat-tern limits their suitability for high-resolution wall-to-wall map-ping. Synthetic aperture radar (SAR) has also been exten-sively used for BHE because of its sensitivity to geometric structure, including single-SAR methods (Recla and Schmitt, 2024), interferometric SAR (InSAR) methods (Zhu et al., 2023;

Afzal et al., 2025), and tomographic SAR (TomoSAR) meth-ods (Chen et al., 2026). Nevertheless, SAR imagery is often aff ected by speckle noise, side-looking distortions such as lay-over and shadows, and limited spectral information, which re-strict its ability to comprehensively characterize complex build-ings (Frantz et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024). To address these limitations, some studies have explored SAR–optical fusion for BHE (Wu et al., 2023; Cao and Weng, 2024). However, the ef-fectiveness of such fusion strategies is still constrained by tem-poral inconsistency and geometric misalignment between mul-timodal data sources, limiting their applicability in large-scale operational mapping. Motivated by the advances of monocular depth estimation (MDE), recent studies have increasingly explored monocular height estimation (MHE) from optical imagery (Esfahani et al., 2025). Compared with stereo-based reconstruction and multi-modal fusion approaches, MHE requires only a single optical image as input, making it a flexible, cost-e ff ective, and scalable solution for regional and global building height mapping. Nev-ertheless, MHE remains highly challenging due to its inherently ill-posed nature (Zhao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). This task is complicated by the indirect and ambiguous relationship be-tween optical image appearance and building height, substan-tial variations in building morphology across cities, and the long-tailed distribution of building heights (Chen et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2025). In addition, shadows, occlusions, back-ground clutter, and spectral confusion between buildings and other land-cover classes may further increase the ambiguity of height inference and degrade prediction accuracy (Srivastava et al., 2017; Song et al., 2026). To address these challenges, ex-isting studies have predominantly relied on deep learning meth-ods, which can learn hierarchical feature representations and complex nonlinear mappings from remote sensing imagery in a data-driven manner. Existing MHE methods can generally be categorized into di-rect regression methods and ordinal regression methods. Di-rect regression methods typically predict continuous height val-ues using encoder–decoder architectures. Mou and Zhu (Mou and Zhu, 2018) were among the first to model the relation-ship between a single optical image and a digital surface model (DSM) using a fully convolutional–deconvolutional network, without introducing any auxiliary supervision. Amirkolaee and Arefi (Amirkolaee and Arefi, 2019) integrated U-Net and ResNet to build a deeper architecture and achieved promising results on three public datasets. Xing et al. (Xing et al., 2021) further enhanced encoder–decoder interaction through a gated feature aggregation module and introduced multi-scale supervi-sion for decoder features. In contrast, ordinal regression methods discretize the height range into ordered bins and combine classification with con-tinuous reconstruction, rather than predicting height as an un-constrained continuous variable. This strategy has been shown to improve optimization stability and prediction quality in MDE, and has gradually been introduced into MHE. Similar to DORN (Fu et al., 2018) in MDE, Li et al. (Li et al., 2020a) were the first to introduce an ordinal encoder–decoder frame-work into MHE, transforming feature embeddings into discrete height maps during decoding and optimizing the network with an ordinal loss. Feng et al. (Feng et al., 2022) further proposed a soft-weighted loss to better convert discrete height intervals into continuous height values by incorporating soft probability distributions. Inspired by AdaBins (Bhat et al., 2021), which adaptively generates depth bins in MDE, HTC-DC Net (Chen et al., 2023c) formulates MHE as a classification–regression task based on a vision transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), while introducing several optimizations tailored to the distribution of height samples. Although HTC-DC Net achieves strong performance, sev-eral aspects still merit further improvement. First, bin-related information is extracted from simply concatenated multi-scale features, which may be insu ffi cient for modeling long-range de-pendencies. Second, bins and bin probabilities are generated from the same feature layer, which may lead to mutual interfer-ence between global and local information. Third, the use of multiple loss terms, including several distribution constraints, may increase the di ffi culty of optimization during training. BinsFormer (Li et al., 2024) addresses similar limitations in MDE by disentangling adaptive bin generation from pixel-wise representation learning and introducing Transformer-based multi-scale refinement for depth prediction. Neverthe-less, directly transferring such designs to BHE may still face several limitations, as bin prediction heads that rely heavily on vision transformers may weaken spatial detail modeling due to the compression of spatial dimensions. More importantly, such frameworks do not explicitly incorporate building footprint su-pervision and may therefore produce over-smoothed building boundaries. This limitation suggests that additional structural guidance is important for accurate and spatially coherent height prediction. Semantic segmentation and height estimation are intrinsi-cally related, because identifying the location, extent, and boundaries of buildings provides explicit structural cues for height prediction. Recent studies have explored multi-task learning frameworks that jointly perform monocular height es-timation and semantic segmentation. Srivastava et al. (Srivas-tava et al., 2017) and FusedSeg-HE (Gültekin et al., 2025) both adopt shared encoder–decoder architectures with task-specific heads to simultaneously predict height and segmentation maps from monocular imagery. Likewise, Liu et al. (Liu et al., 2022) and Gao et al. (Gao et al., 2023) use a shared encoder to learn task-shared representations, separate decoders to extract task-specific features, and feature exchange modules to promote in-formation interaction between the two tasks. In addition, the aforementioned HTC-DC Net introduces an auxiliary binary segmentation objective by treating pixels with heights greater than 1 m as foreground, thereby supervising foreground prob-ability estimation. These studies indicate that incorporating segmentation-related supervision can provide useful structural guidance for height estimation by constraining the spatial extent and boundaries of buildings, thus improving both prediction ac-curacy and spatial coherence. PhiSat-2 (also written as ΦSat-2) is a recently launched mul-tispectral satellite mission operated by the European Space Agency (ESA), providing open-access imagery at 4.75 m spa-2Fig. 1. Compared with widely used open-access optical data sources such as Sentinel-2 and Landsat, PhiSat-2 provides substantially finer spatial resolution, which strength-ens its potential for fine-grained BHE. This combination of enhanced spatial detail and multispectral richness is particu-larly advantageous for urban structural analysis, as it can help characterize building patterns while preserving broader land-cover context. Moreover, its global coverage makes it particu-larly attractive for large-scale BHE. However, the potential of PhiSat-2 imagery for BHE remains underexplored, and dedi-cated datasets and task-oriented methods for its systematic eval-uation are still lacking. Therefore, investigating the suitability of PhiSat-2 for this task is both timely and necessary. To address the above issues, a dedicated dataset, PhiSat-2–Height Dataset (PHDataset) , is constructed for monocular building height estimation based on global PhiSat-2 imagery and open-source building height labels, and a Two-Stream Or-dinal Network (TSONet) is further proposed for this task. The proposed framework integrates footprint-aware structural guid-ance and ordinal height modeling within a unified architec-ture. Specifically, a dual-stream design is adopted to jointly learn footprint segmentation and height estimation, while cross-task feature interaction and multi-level bin refinement are intro-duced to improve structural awareness and ordinal prediction quality. Through this design, more accurate and spatially co-herent building height maps are expected to be generated from PhiSat-2 imagery. In addition, a spatial weighting strategy is incorporated into the L1 regression loss during training to place greater emphasis on spatially informative building regions. The main contributions of this study are summarized as follows: (1) PHDataset is constructed by integrating global PhiSat-2 imagery with open-source building height labels, provid-ing a dedicated dataset for monocular building height es-timation from this new multispectral and high-resolution data source. (2) TSONet is proposed as a two-stream framework in which footprint segmentation and building height estimation are jointly modeled, enabling footprint-aware structural guidance and improved ordinal height prediction through CSEM and FEBR. 3(3) Extensive experiments and additional analyses are con-ducted to validate the proposed dataset and method, while first-ever providing a systematic evaluation of the po-tential and current challenges of PhiSat-2 imagery for monocular building height estimation. 

2. Materials 

To evaluate the capability of PhiSat-2 imagery for BHE, global PhiSat-2 satellite images and open-source building height labels were combined to construct a new dataset, termed 

PHDataset . Data from several sources were first collected and filtered, and then integrated and processed into a machine learn-ing dataset. 

2.1. PhiSat-2 imagery 

According to the ESA mission overview technical note (Longépé et al., 2023), PhiSat-2 is equipped with the Multi-Scape100 visible-to-near-infrared (VNIR) imager, which pro-vides eight spectral bands in total, including seven multispec-tral bands and one additional panchromatic band, with a ground sampling distance of 4.75 m and a swath width of 19.4 km at the reference altitude of 500 km. In this study, only the seven multi-spectral bands were used, as listed in Table 1. Since the payload adopts a push-broom imaging mode, in which di ff erent spectral bands are acquired separately along track, practical PhiSat-2 products may exhibit residual geometric inconsistency, includ-ing spatial o ff sets and inter-band misregistration; these issues will be further discussed in Section 5.3. In this study, PhiSat-2 images acquired between launch and 22 October 2025 were downloaded for several urban areas worldwide. Thirty scenes from 26 cities were retained, as shown in Fig. 1, with low cloud cover and relatively limited geometric distortion. The seven multispectral bands were com-posited and reprojected to the WGS 1984 Web Mercator pro-jection (EPSG:3857). To improve spatial consistency, man-ual georeferencing was further performed using high-resolution Google Earth imagery as reference. Finally, the images were resampled to a spatial resolution of 4.75 m to match the o ffi cial specification. 

2.2. Building height labels 

Open building height labels were compiled from several sources to support global BHE. (1) For regions in North Amer-ica, Australia, and most of Europe, labels were obtained from the Microsoft GlobalMLBuildingFootprints dataset, which pro-vides building-scale height information derived from Bing Maps imagery using deep learning and polygonization tech-niques. (2) For the Vienna region in Europe, labels were ob-tained from the EUBUCCO v0.1 database (Milojevic-Dupont et al., 2023), which contains harmonized building-level height information for Europe retrieved from 50 governmental datasets and OpenStreetMap. (3) For regions in Asia, South America, and Africa, high-resolution and up-to-date open building height labels are not available; therefore, the 3D-GloBFP dataset (Che et al., 2024) was adopted, which provides global building height

2.3. Dataset construction 

To the best of our knowledge, the compiled labels represent among the most recent and highest-quality open building height data currently available at the global scale. Nevertheless, dis-crepancies between the labels and the satellite imagery may still occur due to temporal di ff erences and residual errors, resulting in both omission and commission. To mitigate these issues, valid masks were manually delineated to mark areas with re-liable data coverage, and image patches were generated only within the masked regions. For PHDataset, each pair of PhiSat-2 imagery and building height labels was cropped into patches of 256 × 256 pixels. To increase dataset diversity, 50% of edge patches containing partial NoData pixels and 50% of patches without buildings were retained. All pixel values were stored as float32 . No-Data pixels and label pixels corresponding to non-building ar-eas were set to 0. In total, PHDataset consists of 9 475 co-registered PhiSat-2–label patch pairs, which were randomly split into training, validation, and testing subsets with a ratio of 7:2:1. 

3. Method 

PHDataset consists of high-resolution samples with complex spatial patterns collected from diverse geographic regions, pos-ing challenges to both prediction accuracy and model gener-alization. To address these challenges, a Two-Stream Ordinal Network ( TSONet ) is proposed for BHE. In the following sub-sections, the overall framework is first introduced, followed by the key modules for cross-task feature interaction and ordinal refinement, and finally the training objective, in which a spa-tially weighted L1 loss is adopted to emphasize spatially infor-mative building regions. 4Fig. 2. Overall structure of the proposed Two-Stream Ordinal Network (TSONet). 

3.1. Overall structure 

As illustrated in Fig. 2, TSONet comprises three compo-nents: a shared encoder, a footprint stream, and a height stream. All seven bands of PhiSat-2 imagery are first fed into the shared encoder, which adopts a five-level hierarchical design analo-gous to U-Net to extract multi-level feature representations. The resulting feature pyramid is subsequently delivered to the two task-specific streams. Given the strong correlation between building height estimation and footprint segmentation, a Cross-Stream Exchange Module (CSEM) is introduced to exchange and refine task-related representations between the two streams. Each stream follows a coarse-to-fine decoding hierarchy to progressively recover high-resolution details, where CSEM is applied to refine the highest-resolution features through cross-task interaction. Since footprint segmentation is generally less ambiguous than height estimation, the footprint stream employs a lightweight head with a 3 × 3 convolution to produce foot-print logits. In contrast, the height stream is enhanced by a Feature-Enhanced Bin Refinement (FEBR) module, which fol-lows a classification–regression paradigm to explicitly encode the ordinal structure of building heights. Specifically, FEBR takes features from the top three levels of the height feature pyramid and progressively refines bin queries under the guid-ance of enhanced multi-level features. The refined bin queries also serve as bin representations for subsequent bin prediction. Two Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) are then used to trans-form the final bin representations into (i) bin embeddings and (ii) bin values corresponding to the discretized height bins. The bin embeddings are integrated with the CSEM-refined height features via matrix multiplication to obtain per-pixel bin log-its, representing the probability of each pixel belonging to each height bin. Finally, a continuous height map is obtained by computing the expectation over bins using the predicted bin val-ues and bin logits. Both footprint logits and the height map are jointly supervised through a multi-task objective. 

3.2. Cross-Stream Exchange Module 

The Cross-Stream Exchange Module (CSEM) is designed to enable selective and bidirectional information sharing between footprint segmentation and height estimation, as illustrated in Fig. 3. To improve computational e ffi ciency, group-wise convo-lutions are employed in place of certain standard convolutions. By partitioning channels into multiple groups and performing convolution independently within each group, group-wise con-volution reduces both the number of learnable parameters and the computational cost while preserving su ffi cient channel-wise modeling capacity. Let F f p and Fh denote the footprint and height features at the same spatial resolution, respectively. Directly combining the two streams may introduce interference to footprint-related spatial representations. To preserve fine-grained spatial details and indicate the reliability of exchanged cues, a spatial confi-dence mask is predicted from the footprint feature as:

The grouping strategy for 1 ×1 convo-lutions is determined by their functional roles: group-wise 1 × 1convolutions are used for lightweight channel projection, such as the C → C/M bottleneck inside LRGT, whereas standard 1 × 1 convolutions are retained when full cross-channel inter-action is required for generating control signals and forming exchanged features. 

3.3. Feature-enhanced bin refinement 

To strengthen height estimation, a Feature-Enhanced Bin Re-finement (FEBR) module is introduced at the end of the height stream, as illustrated in Fig. 4. Unlike DETR-like (Carion et al., 2020) bin heads adopted in existing monocular depth and height estimation methods (Bhat et al., 2021; Chen et al., 6Fig. 4. 2023c; Li et al., 2024), where bin queries are mainly refined through stacked self-attention and cross-attention blocks, the proposed FEBR adopts a feature-enhanced refinement strategy. Specifically, multi-level height features are first progressively enhanced and fused, and bin queries are then updated through a Coarse-to-Fine Query Readout (CFQR) process. In this way, bin refinement is explicitly aligned with the hierarchical feature pyramid, rather than relying primarily on repeated attention-based query updates. Such a design is better suited to dense building height estimation, where reliable prediction depends on both large-scale structural context and fine-grained spatial details distributed across di ff erent feature levels. Similar to BinsFormer (Li et al., 2024), FEBR takes the top three levels of the height feature pyramid as inputs and refines bin queries in a coarse-to-fine manner. Starting from the coars-est level, each feature map is first processed by a 1 × 1 convolu-tion for channel alignment. A Restormer block is then applied for feature enhancement and contextual modeling. Compared with standard Transformer blocks used in DETR, Restormer (Zamir et al., 2022) is better suited to high-resolution dense pre-diction due to its more e ffi cient context aggregation and better preservation of local spatial details. At each subsequent level, the enhanced feature from the previous stage is bilinearly up-sampled and fused with the current-level feature by element-wise addition, allowing coarse-level semantic information to be progressively propagated to finer resolutions. Based on the enhanced feature at each level, a Coarse-to-Fine Query Readout (CFQR) module is employed to update the bin queries, thereby progressively refining their feature-conditioned representations. 4.2. Implementation details 

All models are implemented in PyTorch. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU with 16 GB memory. The batch size is set to 10, and the number of training epochs is set to 30. The AdamW optimizer is adopted, with the weight decay set to 0.01. The initial learning rate is set to 1 × 10 −4. A linear warm-up strategy is applied during the first 30% of the training iterations, followed by cosine annealing for learning rate decay. To improve training stability, the maximum 

L2 norm of gradients is clipped to 10. During evaluation, the checkpoint achieving the lowest RMSE on the validation set is selected as the best model.

4.4. Ablation experiments for modules 

Ablation experiments are conducted to evaluate the contri-butions of the proposed CSEM and FEBR modules, and the quantitative results are reported in Table 3. The baseline is defined as the variant without either CSEM or FEBR. When CSEM is removed, no feature exchange is performed between the two streams, and the additional skip connections introduced after the FPNs are also discarded. When FEBR is removed, the DETR-like bin head adopted in BinsFormer (Li et al., 2024) is used for ordinal regression. Overall, both CSEM and FEBR contribute positively to the final performance, while their e ff ects are complementary across height regression and footprint segmentation. Specifically, in-troducing CSEM into the baseline consistently improves the footprint-related metrics, achieving the best REL, IoU, and F1-score among the three ablated variants, which indicates that cross-stream feature exchange is particularly beneficial for en-hancing the structural consistency and segmentation quality of building regions. In contrast, introducing FEBR leads to the lowest RMSE, showing that the proposed feature-enhanced bin refinement strategy is especially e ff ective for improving the overall accuracy of continuous height estimation. When CSEM and FEBR are jointly integrated, the full TSONet achieves the best MAE and Recall, while maintaining highly compet-itive RMSE, REL, IoU, and F1-score. These results suggest that CSEM mainly strengthens cross-task spatial interaction, whereas FEBR primarily improves ordinal height modeling, and their combination yields the most balanced overall perfor-mance. Since height regression and footprint segmentation are optimized jointly, mutual constraints may arise between the two tasks, making it di ffi cult for a single model to simultaneously attain the optimum on all metrics. As a result, the full model does not necessarily achieve the best value on every individual metric, but it provides the most balanced overall performance. Fig. 6 presents qualitative comparisons of the ablation vari-ants in Ankara, Liuzhou, and London, where the red boxes highlight representative local di ff erences. Overall, both CSEM and FEBR contribute positively to the visual quality of height prediction, while their e ff ects exhibit distinct characteristics. Compared with the baseline, introducing CSEM leads to clearer building layouts and more spatially coherent structures, espe-cially in dense urban regions and along building boundaries, which is consistent with its advantage in footprint-related met-rics. In contrast, FEBR produces more plausible height re-sponses and better preserves local height variation, indicating its e ff ectiveness in refining ordinal height prediction. When the two modules are jointly integrated, TSONet yields the most bal-anced qualitative results, with more complete building struc-tures, sharper boundaries, and more faithful height distribu-tions. 4.5. Ablation experiments for task configurations 

To further investigate the roles of ordinal regression and foot-print segmentation in TSONet, ablation experiments under dif-ferent task configurations are conducted. For variants without ordinal regression (bins), the height stream directly outputs the height map after the FPN and a 1 ×1 convolution, without using FEBR or the subsequent MLPs. For variants without footprint segmentation, CSEM is removed accordingly, and only the re-gression loss is retained for training. Table 4 reports the quantitative results of the ablation exper-iments for di ff erent task configurations. Overall, introducing either the bin-based ordinal regression or the footprint segmen-tation task improves the baseline model, while their contribu-tions exhibit di ff erent characteristics. Specifically, adding the bin branch leads to clear improvements in MAE and RMSE, indicating that bin-based ordinal modeling is beneficial for en-hancing the overall accuracy of height estimation. In contrast, adding the footprint branch yields the best REL and Recall, and also improves IoU and F1-score, suggesting that footprint su-pervision is particularly e ff ective in enhancing spatial consis-tency and structural completeness. When the bin branch and the footprint branch are jointly integrated, the full TSONet achieves the best MAE, RMSE, IoU, and F1-score, demon-strating the advantage of combining ordinal height refinement with footprint-aware spatial guidance. It is worth noting that TSONet still does not achieve the best value on every individual metric. A possible reason is that the bin-based ordinal regres-sion obtains the final height map through the expectation opera-tion in Eq. 5. Discussion 

The above results not only demonstrate the potential of PhiSat-2 for monocular building height estimation, but also suggest that the remaining errors are influenced by both model design and data characteristics. In particular, beyond its ad-vantages in spatial detail and multispectral richness, the perfor-mance upper bound is still a ff ected by the pronounced long-tailed height distribution and the current quality challenges of PhiSat-2 products. Therefore, the implications of PhiSat-2 as a data source, together with these two influencing factors, are further discussed below. 

5.1. The advantages of PhiSat-2 imagery for BHE 

The additional analyses in Fig. 8 further help explain why PhiSat-2 is a promising data source for monocular building height estimation. From the spatial perspective, a resolution degradation experiment was conducted to isolate the e ff ect of spatial detail while minimizing interference from other sensor-specific factors. To approximate the coarser spatial scales of widely used open-access Sentinel-2 and Landsat imagery, the original 4.75 m PhiSat-2 data were degraded to 10 m and 30 m equivalents and then resampled back to the original patch size, after which TSONet was trained and evaluated under the same experimental settings. As shown in Fig. 8(a), this process leads to increasingly blurred building boundaries, weaker separation between adjacent structures, and the loss of local roof details. The quantitative results in Fig. 8(b) show a clear trend: as the input resolution becomes coarser, RMSE increases from 7.9477 to 8.2972 and 8.3680, while IoU decreases from 0.3922 to 0.3758 and 0.3463, respectively. These results indicate that the native 4.75 m resolution of PhiSat-2 represents a practically meaningful spatial scale for building-level height inference, as finer spatial detail is essential for preserving structural cues rel-evant to both footprint delineation and building height estima-tion. From the spectral perspective, an input band configuration experiment was conducted to examine the contribution of mul-tispectral information in PhiSat-2 imagery. Specifically, the model was separately trained and evaluated using all seven bands, RGB +NIR, and RGB inputs under the same experimen-tal settings. As shown in Fig. 8(c), the full seven-band input achieves the best overall performance, while reducing the in-put to RGB +NIR or RGB leads to increased RMSE and lower IoU overall. This result suggests that the advantage of PhiSat-2 does not rely on spatial resolution alone, but also benefits from multispectral observations that provide complementary cues beyond standard RGB imagery. At the same time, the non-strictly monotonic change in IoU between RGB +NIR and RGB indicates that the contribution of additional bands is not purely additive, but depends on how the spectral information is jointly exploited by the model.

Taken together, these results indicate that the value of PhiSat-2 for monocular BHE lies in its balanced combination of building-relevant spatial detail and multispectral richness, in addition to its open accessibility and global coverage. This combination distinguishes it from conventional open optical products with coarser spatial resolution, while also making it potentially more informative than high-resolution RGB-only imagery for large-scale urban vertical mapping. 

5.2. Long-tailed height distribution 

As shown in Fig. 9, the distribution of ground-truth build-ing heights exhibits a pronounced long-tailed pattern in both the training and test sets. Most valid building pixels are con-centrated in the low-rise range, especially below approximately 30 m, whereas the proportion of high-rise samples decreases rapidly with increasing height. The close agreement between the training and test distributions indicates that the two sub-sets share a similar overall height profile, suggesting that the observed error trends are not caused by an evident mismatch between training and testing distributions. The bottom panel further shows that the RMSE increases consistently with ground-truth height for all compared meth-ods, indicating that taller buildings are substantially more dif-ficult to estimate than low-rise ones. This trend is closely re-lated to the severe imbalance in height samples, as the scarcity of high-rise pixels provides less e ff ective supervision for learn-ing reliable height representations in the upper range. More-over, the performance gap between methods becomes more ev-ident in the medium- and high-height intervals. Compared with HTC-DC Net and BinsFormer, which are representative MHE and MDE methods, TSONet achieves lower RMSE across most height bins, and its advantage remains visible even for relatively tall buildings, suggesting that the proposed method is more ro-bust under long-tailed height distributions. Nevertheless, the error still increases markedly for high-rise buildings, implying that the long-tail e ff ect remains a major challenge for reliable monocular building height estimation from PhiSat-2 imagery. 14 Fig. 9. 5.3. Challenges associated with PhiSat-2 products for BHE 

As shown in Fig. 10, the currently available PhiSat-2 imagery products still exhibit several geometric challenges that may af-fect monocular building height estimation. At the scene level, the nominal image extent does not always fully match the ac-tual valid coverage, indicating noticeable spatial o ff sets and ge-ometric inconsistency in the provided products. At the local level, the enlarged example further reveals evident inter-band misregistration and geometric deformation, as the same build-ing structures are not perfectly aligned across di ff erent spectral bands. These issues may produce color fringing and blurred structural boundaries in the RGB composite, while also making it more di ffi cult to accurately align the imagery with the corre-sponding labels. As noted in Section 2.1, this is also consistent with the push-broom imaging mode of PhiSat-2, in which dif-ferent spectral bands are acquired separately along track and may therefore remain a ff ected by residual band-to-band mis-registration in practical products. Such challenges are particularly relevant to building height estimation, because this task depends strongly on fine-scale structural cues, including roof contours, boundary sharpness, and the separation between adjacent buildings. Once these cues are degraded by spatial o ff sets, local deformation, and band-wise inconsistency, the model tends to generate smoother build-ing outlines and less reliable height predictions, especially for narrow buildings, dense urban blocks, and complex roof struc-tures. 6. Conclusion 

In this study, the potential of PhiSat-2 imagery for monoc-ular building height estimation was explored for the first time. 15 Specifically, PHDataset was constructed by integrating global seven-band PhiSat-2 imagery with open-source building height labels, resulting in 9,475 co-registered patch pairs collected from diverse urban scenes worldwide. Building upon this dataset, TSONet was proposed as a multi-task framework to jointly perform building height estimation and footprint seg-mentation. With the aid of CSEM for selective cross-task fea-ture exchange and FEBR for coarse-to-fine ordinal query refine-ment, TSONet e ff ectively combines footprint-aware structural guidance with feature-enhanced ordinal height modeling. Ex-perimental results show that TSONet consistently outperforms representative methods and achieves the best overall perfor-mance on PHDataset, yielding the lowest MAE (4.9925) and RMSE (7.9477), together with the highest IoU (0.3922) and F1-score (0.5635) among all compared methods. The ablation results further confirm that CSEM mainly benefits structural consistency and footprint segmentation quality, whereas FEBR is more e ff ective in improving height regression accuracy, and their joint use leads to the most balanced performance. In addi-tion, the ablation study on di ff erent task configurations verifies the e ff ectiveness of jointly introducing ordinal regression and footprint segmentation, further demonstrating the advantage of the proposed framework. Overall, this work demonstrates the strong potential of PhiSat-2 imagery for large-scale urban vertical mapping. Ad-ditional analyses further indicate that the value of PhiSat-2 for monocular BHE lies not only in its open accessibility and global coverage, but also in its combination of building-relevant spa-tial detail and multispectral richness. At the same time, the discussion suggests that the remaining errors are still strongly aff ected by the long-tailed distribution of building heights and the native geometric challenges of PhiSat-2 product. Future work will therefore focus not only on expanding the dataset with more high-quality samples across broader geographic re-gions to improve model robustness and generalization, but also on improving data quality control and preprocessing to miti-gate the influence of geometric inconsistencies. Meanwhile, considering the scarcity of accurate and temporally aligned la-bels, automatic label generation and more label-e ffi cient learn-ing strategies, such as weakly supervised and semi-supervised learning, should also be explored.
