## Abstract 

Vision Foundation Models (VFM) pre-trained on large-scale unlabeled data have achieved remarkable success on general computer vision tasks, yet typically suffer from significant domain gaps when applied to agricul-ture. In this context, we introduce SPROUT (Scalable 

Plant Representation model via Open-field Unsupervised 

Training), a multi-crop, multi-task agricultural founda-tion model trained via diffusion denoising. SPROUT leverages a VAE-free Pixel-space Diffusion Transformer to learn rich, structure-aware representations through de-noising and enabling efficient end-to-end training. We pre-train SPROUT on a curated dataset of 2.6 million high-quality agricultural images spanning diverse crops, growth stages, and environments. Extensive experiments demonstrate that SPROUT consistently outperforms state-of-the-art web-pretrained and agricultural foundation mod-els across a wide range of downstream tasks, while re-quiring substantially lower pre-training cost. The code and model are available at https://github.com/UTokyo-FieldPhenomics-Lab/SPROUT. 

## 1. Introduction 

Deep learning has fundamentally reshaped computer vision, largely due to the reliance of supervised learning on massive amounts of manually labeled data. While supervised meth-ods excel in many areas, the high cost of annotation and the need for specialized knowledge make the acquisition of large-scale labeled data a bottleneck. Furthermore, super-vised methods often struggle to utilize orders of magnitude 1

Unsupervised and weakly-supervised learning offer a compelling alternative by learning visual representations directly from unlabeled data. Recent advances have led to powerful Vision Foundation Models (VFMs), such as CLIP [36], DINOv2 [30], and DINOv3 [42], which are pre-trained on massive collections of internet images and exhibit strong transferability and zero-shot performance on standard benchmarks like ImageNet. These models signifi-cantly reduce reliance on labeled data and have become the backbone of modern vision systems. Despite their success, general-purpose VFMs often suf-fer from substantial domain gaps when applied to highly specialized vertical domains. In such settings, extensive fine-tuning on domain-specific labeled data is typically re-quired, undermining the original motivation for using foun-dation models. Consequently, fields such as biomedicine [8, 35], remote sensing [47], and astronomy [31] have in-creasingly turned toward training domain-specific founda-tion models tailored to their unique data characteristics and task requirements. In contrast, the development of agricultural foundation models remains comparatively underexplored. While sev-eral recent works have begun to investigate agricultural pre-training [3, 13, 41], existing approaches are often restricted to a single crop or a narrow set of tasks. More importantly, they largely adopt self-supervised learning paradigms orig-inally designed for web-scale, object-centric imagery, with-out accounting for the unique visual properties of agricul-tural scenes. Current unsupervised representation learning predomi-nantly follows three paradigms: (1) Contrastive Learning (e.g., SimCLR [7]), which aligns representations of aug-mented views while pushing apart negative samples; (2) Self-Distillation (e.g., DINO [5]), which employs a teacher-student framework to match teacher and student embed-dings; and (3) Masked Image Modeling (MIM) (e.g., MAE [16], BEiT [2], iBOT [53]), which reconstructs masked patches from visible context. Contrastive learning and self-distillation rely heavily on random cropping to form posi-tive and negative pairs or whole-part views. These cropped views enable models to learn the semantic distinctions be-tween objects and their constituent parts. Masked im-age modeling, in contrast, operates within a single view. However, existing formulations either introduce substan-tial architectural complexity (e.g., the discrete variational autoencoder in BEiT) or fall short of state-of-the-art self-supervised methods Agricultural images differ fundamentally from natural internet images. They are predominantly texture-centric and characterized by densely packed plants with repetitive patterns, subtle structural variations, and strong correlations across spatial scales. While random cropping is effective for isolated objects, this strategy often becomes ineffective in agricultural scenes because it captures visually similar textures from different plants, thereby influencing semantic and structural representation learning. 2Motivated by these challenges, we revisit diffusion-based generative models as an alternative approach to agri-cultural representation learning. Diffusion models learn to reverse a noise corruption process through denoising, a label-free objective that naturally encourages the model to capture fine-grained structure. Recent progress in Transformer-based diffusion architectures (DiT) [34] has revealed strong scaling behavior in generative modeling, suggesting their potential as powerful unsupervised rep-resentation learners. Unlike contrastive objectives, diffu-sion denoising does not depend on cropping-based view construction, making it particularly well suited for texture-dominated agricultural imagery. Building on this insight, we propose SPROUT (Scalable 

Plant Representation model via Open-field Unsupervised 

Training), the first multi-crop, multi-task agricultural vision foundation model pre-trained via diffusion denoising. We introduce a VAE-free Diffusion Transformer (UDiT) that operates directly in pixel space, enabling end-to-end opti-mization and significantly improving training efficiency by avoiding the computational overhead of latent VAEs. To further tailor diffusion models for discriminative represen-tation learning, we systematically study design choices such as noise parameterization and architecture scaling. In addition, we address a key practical challenge in diffusion-based representation learning: timestep selection. Instead of relying on exhaustive downstream evaluation or heuristic choices, we propose a simple, training-free cri-terion based on the effective rank of diffusion features to identify the most informative timesteps. This principled selection strategy consistently yields representations with stronger transferability. To support large-scale agricultural pre-training, we cu-rate a diverse dataset of 2.6 million high-quality agricultural images, distilled from an initial pool of 4 million samples collected over a decade across multiple locations, years, and crop varieties. This dataset captures rich variations in plant structure, growth stages, and environmental conditions, pro-viding a strong foundation for learning general plant repre-sentations. Extensive experiments demonstrate that SPROUT learns powerful and transferable representations across a wide range of agricultural tasks. In particular, SPROUT ex-cels at dense prediction problems such as organ-level and plant-level segmentation, depth estimation, and counting, where structural understanding is critical. It consistently outperforms state-of-the-art foundation models pre-trained on web-scale data, while also achieving competitive results on classification tasks. Notably, SPROUT delivers these gains with substantially lower pre-training cost compared to existing agricultural foundation models. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: • We propose a novel pre-training framework based on Pixel-space Diffusion Transformers. By eliminating the VAE and introducing an Effective Rank-based timestep selection method, we enable efficient learning of robust, structurally aware representations. • We present a rigorous data curation pipeline and release a large-scale agricultural dataset consisting of 2.6 million high-quality images, covering diverse crops, regions, and environmental conditions. • We introduce SPROUT , the first scalable, diffusion-based agricultural vision foundation model capable of multi-crop and multi-task transfer. • Through extensive experimentation, we demonstrate that SPROUT achieves state-of-the-art performance on a vari-ety of agricultural benchmarks, especially for dense and structure-sensitive tasks, while significantly reducing the computational cost.

2.2. Removing DiT’s Dependency on VAE 

Following LDM [38], DiT [33] encodes the image from the pixel space to the latent space using a VAE [19], and then trains within the latent space. However, VAEs lead to the separation of the model training: the latency of VAE is not optimal for subsequent image generation or downstream tasks, and VAE cannot be jointly optimized end-to-end with diffusion models. Several recent works have explored the feasibility of training DiT end-to-end directly in pixel space. JiT [22] discovered that, when training DiT in pixel space, convergence is only achieved through the x0 parame-terization. Predicting either the ϵ or the v leads to optimiza-tion instability. This is because x0 lies on a low-dimensional manifold, while the ϵ and v span the full high-dimensional pixel space. This makes them harder to model. However, this conclusion is drawn from a generative modeling perspective and does not extend to representation learning. We contend that ϵ is a more effective representa-tion learning objective because it has a full-rank distribution over pixel space. The task of predicting ϵ therefore neces-sitates high-rank internal representations, serving as a regu-larizer that retains fine-grained details and boosts capacity. Thus, our goal is to utilize ϵ parameterization in pixel space without sacrificing stability. To enable stable training with ϵ or v parameterization in pixel space, recent methods have proposed different strate-gies. DSD stabilizes the joint training of the pixel en-coder and diffusion model via a self-distillation mechanism. DeCo, on the other hand, augments DiT with a pixel de-coder: while the former adopts 16 × 16 patches, the latter uses 1 × 1 patches to directly encode noisy images, preserv-ing high-frequency information through fine-grained patch-ing and long-range interactions, which leads to stable con-vergence. To this end, prior work introduces stabilization strate-gies. DSD [49] employs self-distillation to jointly train the pixel encoder and diffusion model, while DeCo [27] aug-ments DiT with a pixel decoder and fine-grained 1×1 patch-ing to preserve high-frequency information and ensure con-vergence. Despite their effectiveness, these methods rely on patchify/unpatchify operations to map between spatial lay-outs and token channels. We argue that forcing the DiT to map token channels back to spatial layouts wastes model

2.3. Efficient Timestep Selection via Effective Rank 

When applying diffusion models to discriminative tasks, the timestep t is a critical hyperparameter that determines the characteristics of the learned representations. Early timesteps (high noise levels) typically produce abstract but blurry features, while later timesteps (low noise levels) em-phasize fine-grained structure and high-frequency textures, often at the cost of semantic abstraction. Selecting an ap-propriate timestep is therefore essential for obtaining high-quality representations. Despite its importance, most existing work often lacks guidance on the choice of t. Existing strategies are typically either: 1. Exhaustive Search: Evaluating the model on down-stream datasets across all timesteps, which is computation-ally slow and costly. 2. Heuristic Selection: Fixing a specific timestep based on empirical intuition, which fails to generalize across dif-ferent data distributions. 2.4. Dataset Construction and Curation 

We curate a large-scale agricultural dataset of 4.3 million images, combining 2.4 million proprietary samples col-lected over a decade with the web-crawled data. Inspired by recent approaches (DINO V2, MOVIEGEN), we imple-ment a three-stage filtration pipeline: Visual Quality Filtering. We apply a series of filters to remove images with low visual fidelity, eliminating approx-imately 250K samples. This stage eliminates exposure and utilizes binary classifiers to remove samples exhibiting sig-nificant fog or blur. Feature-based Filtering. To ensure dataset diversity and informational density, we employ embedding-based tech-niques. We utilize SSCD model to compute image embed-dings and remove near-duplicates based on cosine similar-ity. Furthermore, to eliminate samples with low semantic content (e.g., clear skies or uniform backgrounds), we ex-tract patch-level features using DINOv2, and filter based on the variance of the features. In this stage, 1.2M images were filtered out. Content Filtering. ## 3. Experiments 

To thoroughly assess SPROUT’s performance in agricul-tural vision, we assemble a large collection of publicly available agricultural datasets spanning diverse tasks and crop species, and compare it against representative base-lines. 

3.1. SPROUT Produces Outstanding Dense Repre-sentations 

In this section, we assess the quality of the dense representa-tions learned by SPROUT. We compare SPROUT with the most advanced visual encoders trained on large-scale web datasets. These models fall into three categories: Masked Image Modeling (MAE, MSN), self-distillation (DINOv2, DINOv3), and contrastive learning (CLIP, SigLIP). Ad-ditionally, we include FOMO4Wheat, a DINOv2-based model specifically trained on wheat images, as a domain-specific baseline. In the quantitative evaluation, all models except FOMO4Wheat do not use auxiliary decoders. Instead, a sin-gle convolutional layer is applied to project the final output features of each model. During fine-tuning, all model pa-rameters are updated. For FOMO4Wheat, a Mask2Former decoder was adopted. FFine-tuning and inference are per-formed at a resolution of 256 × 256 , and sliding window inference is used for high-resolution images.

3.1.4 Comparison with Single-crop Foundational Model 

We compare our proposed method, SPROUT, with the FOMO4Wheat[13], a recently introduced agricultural foun-dation model focusing on wheat. FOMO4Wheat directly uses the DINOv2 framework and is pre-trained on 2.5 mil-lion wheat images. To ensure a fair comparison, we use the same evaluation protocol as FOMO4Wheat. This involves re-splitting the GWFSS dataset [50] an 8:2 training and test-ing set, rather than using the official competition split. Fur-thermore, all models were initialized randomly prior to pre-training to avoid reliance on external weights. As shown in Table 3, our smallest variant, UDiT-S, achieves performance comparable to the largest FOMO4Wheat model (ViT-G) while utilizing approxi-mately 1/22 of the parameters. As model capacity in-creases, UDiT-L significantly outperforms FOMO4Wheat, with particularly clear improvements on the most challeng-ing category, Stem , which requires fine-grained understand-ing of plant structure. Moreover, our approach requires a substantially lower computational cost. These results un-derscore both the efficiency and effectiveness of our pre-training strategy for agricultural data. 

3.1.5 Depth Estimation 

Depth estimation provides dense geometric information about crops, which is essential for understanding canopy structure and monitoring growth dynamics. Depth cues are also a key component for enabling agricultural robots to perceive their environment. We evaluate the proposed model on the sugar beet depth dataset [6], which provides real depth maps captured by Kinect v2. Quantitative results are reported in Table 4. SPROUT achieves the lowest error across all evaluation metrics, indicating more accurate depth prediction. Qual-itative visualization results are shown in Fig. 4. Predictions from other models tend to exhibit less coherent geometric structure, especially around thin leaf edges. In contrast, SPROUT produces depth maps with sharper boundaries and more detailed plant structures.

3.3.2 Scaling the Pre-training Dataset Size 

We further study how fine-tuning performance evolves as the volume of unlabeled pre-training data increases. Fig.6 demonstrates that, as the unlabeled dataset is scaled up, downstream performance improves steadily when the dataset size is below 64K samples. However, once the dataset reaches approximately 64K, a clear satura-tion point emerges: further increasing the amount of homo-geneous data yields diminishing returns. This observation suggests that future dataset construction should prioritize data diversity over simply increasing volume. In practice, more aggressive deduplication strategies can be adopted, and efforts should be made to collect data from diverse en-vironments, crop types, and acquisition conditions. 

3.3.3 Estimation of the Optimal Number of Training Iterations 

To identify optimal training strategies, we explore the iter-ations required for SPROUT-L to reach convergence across different dataset sizes. As shown in the rightmost plot of Fig. 6, we find that the convergence training steps scale lin-early with the square root of the dataset size. Therefore: 

For every four-fold increase in dataset size, the pre-training computational budget should be doubled. 

This empirical scaling rule offers practical guidance for allocating computing resources. For a new dataset, we can first extract a very small subset, calculate the number of steps required for convergence, and estimate the computa-tional cost for convergence of the entire dataset. 

3.3.4 Comparison with Other Unsupervised Learning Methods 

For comparison, we pre-train models on the MCD 2.6M dataset using MAE and DINOv2. Under the same ex-perimental settings, SPROUT converges significantly faster than both MAE and DINOv2, highlighting its superior training efficiency. 

3.3.5 Efficient Utilization of Labeled Data 

Finally, we evaluate how effectively SPROUT leverages labeled data during fine-tuning. As illustrated in Fig. 7, SPROUT surpasses DINOv2 while using only 1/50 of the labeled data, demonstrating its strong label efficiency and practical advantage in low-annotation regimes. 

## 4. Conclusion 

We develop a diffusion-based pre-training framework tai-lored to agricultural imagery. Building on this framework, we introduce SPROUT, an agricultural vision foundation model pre-trained via pixel-space denoising. Extensive ex-periments demonstrate consistent improvements over exist-ing VFMs across a wide range of agricultural tasks, particu-larly for dense and structure-sensitive predictions, while re-quiring substantially lower pre-training cost. SPROUT thus provides a practical foundation for accurate, scalable, and label-efficient agricultural vision systems.
